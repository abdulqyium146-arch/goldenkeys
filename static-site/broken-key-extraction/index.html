What to Do (and NOT Do) When a Key Breaks

✅ DO These Things

  • Stop turning immediately when you feel resistance
  • Leave the fragment as undisturbed as possible
  • Note how far the fragment is inside the keyway
  • Check if the lock is in the locked or unlocked position
  • Call a professional locksmith

❌ DON'T Do These Things

  • Don't try to push the fragment in further with the key stub
  • Don't use superglue — it contaminates the keyway and ruins the cylinder
  • Don't use tweezers blindly — pushing the fragment deeper
  • Don't try to drill the fragment — damages the cylinder
  • Don't force the remaining key stub into the lock

How We Extract Broken Keys

1

Assess Fragment Position

Using a small flashlight and inspection probe, we determine how far the key fragment is inside the keyway and its orientation. This guides tool selection.

2

Lubricate the Keyway

Dry lubricant (graphite or Teflon spray) is applied to reduce friction and help the fragment slide out smoothly. This also protects the tumbler pins during extraction.

3

Insert Extraction Tools

Spiral extraction hooks or curved probe extractors are inserted alongside the key fragment, engaging the serrations on the key blade. Gentle outward pressure combined with wiggling dislodges the fragment.

4

Extract and Inspect

Fragment removed. Cylinder tested for smooth operation. We check for any tumbler damage. If the cylinder is damaged, we discuss replacement options. Then we cut a new key from the extracted fragment (if complete enough) or from the lock's code.

When the Cylinder Needs Replacement

In some cases — particularly when superglue has been used, the fragment is deeply embedded, or the keyway is damaged — cylinder replacement is more practical than extraction. We'll tell you honestly which approach applies to your situation before billing any work.

After Extraction: Why Did the Key Break?

We always diagnose the underlying cause after extraction. Common findings:

  • Worn cylinder: Tight tumblers causing extra torque stress — we recommend rekeying or replacing
  • Key fatigue: Old key metal fatigued from repeated use — new key cut from the original profile
  • Frozen lock: Ice or corrosion binding the cylinder — treatment recommended to prevent recurrence
