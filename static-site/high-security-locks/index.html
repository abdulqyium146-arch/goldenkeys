What Makes a Lock "High-Security"

🛡

Pick Resistance

Standard pin tumbler locks can be picked in seconds by skilled intruders. High-security locks add security pins (spool, serrated, mushroom) and sidebar mechanisms that make single-pin picking essentially impossible without specialized knowledge and hours of time.

🔌

Bump Resistance

Bump keys can open most standard locks in under a second. High-security locks counter this with anti-bump pins that require precise timing virtually impossible to achieve, or sidebar locks that physically prevent bump technique.

🔧

Drill Resistance

Hardened steel inserts at critical points in the cylinder (around the shear line and cam) shatter drill bits and prevent bypass via drilling. Standard cylinders can be drilled in under 2 minutes with a cordless drill.

🔐

Key Control

Patented restricted keyways prevent key blanks from being obtained at hardware stores. Only authorized dealers with proper ID can duplicate keys. This patent protection lasts 5–15 years depending on the brand.

High-Security Lock FAQ

True high-security locks combine four elements: (1) pick resistance via security pins or sidebar mechanisms, (2) bump resistance via anti-bump pins, (3) drill resistance via hardened steel inserts, and (4) key control via patented restricted keyways that prevent unauthorized duplication. All four must be present to qualify as truly high-security.

High-security lock hardware costs $80–$300 per lock. Installed price runs $150–$500 per door depending on brand and whether new drilling is required. Medeco Maxum retails at $150–$250 hardware only; Mul-T-Lock MT5+ at $120–$200 hardware only.

For most residential doors, a Grade 1 Schlage B60N ($65–$150 installed) is excellent security. High-security locks justify the premium when you need key control (rental properties), insurance mandates UL-listed hardware, or you're in a high-value commercial environment. The 25–40 year lifespan also makes them cost-effective long-term.

Medeco uses angled key cuts that rotate pins plus a sidebar mechanism. Mul-T-Lock uses a pin-in-pin (telescoping pins) design. Both provide excellent pick, bump, and drill resistance with patented key control. Medeco is more common in residential; Mul-T-Lock dominates commercial/government applications.

High-security locks are specifically engineered to resist bump keys. Medeco's angled pins cannot be bumped using standard techniques. Mul-T-Lock's pin-in-pin design requires extremely precise timing. ASSA Abloy's Protec2 uses a secondary locking bar that physically prevents bump entry.
